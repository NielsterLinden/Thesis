\chapter{Best Input Representation}
\label{ch:input-repr}

Before the transformer processes a single attention operation, several choices have already determined the information it receives.
These choices concern \emph{how} each particle is represented as a token, and \emph{what} the token sequence contains.
This chapter evaluates six such choices — the tokenizer family (\wandbaxis{T1}), the particle-identity (PID) embedding mode (\wandbaxis{T1-a}), the PID embedding dimension (\wandbaxis{T1-b}), the kinematic feature set (\wandbaxis{D01}), whether missing transverse energy enters as an additional token (\wandbaxis{D02}), and the ordering of tokens in the input sequence (\wandbaxis{D03}) — and asks which of them affect classification performance on the 4-tops-versus-background task.

The chapter is structured as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:ch4-setup} describes the shared experimental setup and evaluation metric.
Section~\ref{sec:ch4-tokenizer} presents the tokenizer family comparison, which is the primary result of this chapter.
Section~\ref{sec:ch4-pid} examines the sub-choices within the identity tokenizer (T1-a and T1-b).
Section~\ref{sec:ch4-data} covers the data-treatment axes (D01, D02, D03).
Section~\ref{sec:ch4-discussion} synthesises the findings and establishes the baseline configuration carried forward into subsequent chapters.

The one-sentence answer is: the tokenizer family dominates all other choices, with the identity tokenizer outperforming kinematics-only alternatives by a large margin; fine-grained choices within that family and all data-treatment axes are null or near-null results at this model scale.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:ch4-setup}
% ============================================================

Two experiments are reported here.
\textbf{Experiment~4A} sweeps the tokenizer family (\wandbaxis{T1}) and, within the identity family, the PID embedding mode (\wandbaxis{T1-a}) and embedding dimension (\wandbaxis{T1-b}).
\textbf{Experiment~4B} fixes the tokenizer at identity and sweeps the three data-treatment axes (\wandbaxis{D01}, \wandbaxis{D02}, \wandbaxis{D03}) in a full $2 \times 2 \times 2$ factorial design.

All axes not under study are held at canonical defaults throughout:
positional encoding (\wandbaxis{E1}) is sinusoidal in model space;
attention biases (B1) are disabled;
pooling (C2) uses a CLS token;
the FFN (F1) is the standard implementation.
The model size is the \texttt{d64\_L4} baseline (H01\,=\,64, H02\,=\,4), used uniformly across all runs in this chapter.
Every condition is replicated with three seeds (R05 $\in$ \{42, 123, 314\}).

The primary evaluation metric is the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) computed on the held-out test set.
All metric values are drawn from \texttt{eval\_v2/test\_auroc} in the W\&B export, which is recorded at training time and is not affected by the subsequent re-inference step.
Reported means and standard deviations are over the three seeds unless stated otherwise; with $n=3$ seeds per condition, these figures are indicative and should not be interpreted as tight confidence intervals.

\section{Tokenizer Family}
\label{sec:ch4-tokenizer}
% ============================================================

The choice of tokenizer family (\wandbaxis{T1}) is the most consequential design decision examined in this chapter.
Three families are compared.
The \texttt{raw} tokenizer maps each particle's continuous kinematics $(E, p_T, \eta, \phi)$ directly to the model dimension via a shared linear projection; no particle-type information is provided.
The \texttt{identity} tokenizer augments the kinematic projection with an explicit PID embedding, which encodes the discrete particle type label (jet, b-jet, electron, muon, photon, etc.) and concatenates it with the projected kinematics.
The \texttt{binned} tokenizer discards continuous features entirely, representing each particle solely by its histogram-binned kinematic cell index as a discrete token.

Figure~\ref{fig:auroc-bar-tokenizer} shows the mean test AUROC for each family.
The \texttt{identity} tokenizer achieves a mean AUROC of approximately $0.842$ (evaluated on cells with embedding dimension $\geq 16$, where the learned-mode confound described in Section~\ref{sec:ch4-pid} is absent; $n=21$ seeds), compared with $0.804 \pm 0.001$ for \texttt{raw} ($n=3$) and $0.725 \pm 0.002$ for \texttt{binned} ($n=3$).
The gap between \texttt{identity} and \texttt{raw} is $+0.038$ AUROC; the gap between \texttt{identity} and \texttt{binned} is $+0.117$.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{figures/ch4/figure-auroc_bar_by_tokenizer.pdf}
\caption{Mean test AUROC by tokenizer family (T1).
Error bars show one standard deviation over seeds.
The \texttt{identity} bar pools cells with embedding dimension $\geq 16$ ($n=21$ seeds) to exclude the \texttt{learned}/dim=8 confound discussed in Section~\ref{sec:ch4-pid}.
\texttt{raw} and \texttt{binned} each have $n=3$.}
\label{fig:auroc-bar-tokenizer}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:roc-tokenizer} shows the ROC curves for each family.
The identity advantage is present uniformly across the full operating range: at every background rejection threshold, the identity tokenizer achieves higher signal efficiency than the raw tokenizer, and both substantially outperform the binned tokenizer.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{figures/ch4/figure-roc_curves_by_tokenizer.pdf}
\caption{ROC curves by tokenizer family (T1).
Individual seed curves are shown for \texttt{raw} and \texttt{binned}; the mean curve is shown for \texttt{identity}.
The performance ordering is consistent across all background rejection values.}
\label{fig:roc-tokenizer}
\end{figure}

The training dynamics in Figure~\ref{fig:val-auroc-tokenizer} add interpretive context.
The \texttt{binned} tokenizer reaches a validation AUROC peak near epoch~4 and subsequently collapses, behaviour consistent with rapid memorisation of the discrete token vocabulary followed by overfitting once no additional generalisation signal remains.
The \texttt{raw} tokenizer converges stably but plateaus at a lower level.
The \texttt{identity} tokenizer converges to a higher plateau with a gradual, mild decline beyond the optimum, consistent with moderate overtraining over 50 epochs.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{figures/ch4/figure-val_auroc_by_tokenizer.pdf}
\caption{Validation AUROC over training epochs by tokenizer family (T1).

\texttt{raw} converges stably but at a lower plateau.
\texttt{identity} converges to the highest plateau and degrades only slowly beyond the optimum.
The x-axis is cropped to 30 epochs to focus on the convergence region.}
\label{fig:val-auroc-tokenizer}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The result is consistent with a physics-motivated expectation: particle type carries information that is not fully recoverable from the four-vector alone, particularly for particles with overlapping kinematic distributions (e.g.\ light jets and b-jets at similar $p_T$ and $\eta$).
By providing an explicit PID label, the identity tokenizer allows the model to apply type-specific attention patterns and feature combinations that the raw tokenizer cannot form.
The binned tokenizer is the weakest family because it discards not only the type label but also the continuous kinematic resolution; only coarse bin membership remains.

\subsection{Failure Analysis: Where PID Changes the Outcome}
\label{sec:ch4-failure}
% -------------------------------------------------------

To quantify where the tokenizer gap originates, each event in the test set ($N=30{,}207$) is classified by both the best \texttt{raw} model (AUROC\,=\,0.8056) and the best \texttt{identity} model (\texttt{one\_hot}, AUROC\,=\,0.8451), using a threshold of 0.5 on the signal score.\footnote{W\&B run identifiers: raw — \texttt{run\_20260410-160141\_ch4\_input\_repr\_exp4a\_1\_raw\_job002}; identity — \texttt{run\_20260410-162037\_ch4\_input\_repr\_exp4a\_2\_job010}.}

Figure~\ref{fig:failure-analysis} shows the per-event outcome breakdown.
Of the $30{,}207$ test events, $20{,}289$ (67.2\%) are classified correctly by both tokenizers, and $5{,}396$ (17.9\%) are wrong for both, defining an irreducible difficulty floor that reflects events where neither tokenizer has sufficient information to classify correctly.
The remaining events reveal the tokenizer effect:
$2{,}713$ events (9.0\%) are correctly classified by the identity tokenizer but not by the raw tokenizer (PID helped),
while $1{,}809$ events (6.0\%) are correctly classified by the raw tokenizer but not by the identity tokenizer (PID hurt).
The net gain from the explicit PID label is $+904$ events, or $+3.0\%$ of the test set.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ch4/figure-failure_analysis_raw_vs_identity.pdf}
\caption{Per-event outcome comparison between the best \texttt{raw} and best \texttt{identity} tokenizer models on the test set ($N=30{,}207$).
\textit{Left:} scatter of per-event signal scores; colours indicate outcome category.
Events above the diagonal (identity score $>$ raw score) tend to benefit from the PID label.
\textit{Right:} fraction of events in each category.
The net asymmetry (9.0\% PID-helped vs.\ 6.0\% PID-hurt) accounts for the identity tokenizer's overall AUROC advantage.}
\label{fig:failure-analysis}
\end{figure}

The 6.0\% of events where the raw tokenizer outperforms the identity tokenizer warrants a brief remark.
These events are not evidence that PID is harmful in general; rather, they reflect the stochastic nature of threshold classification and the fact that no tokenizer achieves perfect classification on all events.
For some events, the kinematic features alone may provide a cleaner signal than the combination of kinematics and a noisy or ambiguous type label.

\subsection{PID Embedding Mode and Dimension}
\label{sec:ch4-pid}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Within the identity tokenizer family, two further choices determine how the PID label enters the model: the embedding mode (\wandbaxis{T1-a}) and the embedding dimension (\wandbaxis{T1-b}).
This subsection examines these sub-choices, which have a substantially smaller effect than the tokenizer family itself.

Figure~\ref{fig:auroc-bar-pid-mode} shows the mean test AUROC by PID embedding mode, pooled over all embedding dimensions.
The marginal means are: \texttt{one\_hot} $= 0.8442 \pm 0.0006$ ($n=3$), \texttt{fixed\_random} $= 0.8410 \pm 0.0021$ ($n=9$), \texttt{learned} $= 0.8420 \pm 0.0017$ ($n=27$).
The apparent underperformance of \texttt{learned} relative to \texttt{one\_hot} in this marginal view is a confound: the \texttt{learned} sample is dominated by dimension-8 runs, which perform anomalously.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{figures/ch4/figure-auroc_bar_by_pid_mode.pdf}
\caption{Mean test AUROC by PID embedding mode (T1-a), pooled over all T1-b embedding dimensions.
The apparent gap for \texttt{learned} reflects the dominance of dimension-8 cells in the \texttt{learned} sample.
Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap-pid-dim} shows that at dimension $\geq 16$, all three modes are within $0.003$ AUROC of each other.}
\label{fig:auroc-bar-pid-mode}
\end{figure}

The heatmap in Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap-pid-dim} resolves this confound by showing AUROC as a joint function of mode and dimension.
The per-cell results from Experiment~4A (D01\,=\,\texttt{[0,1,2,3]}, D02\,=\,\texttt{false}, D03\,=\,\texttt{input\_order}) are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{learned}, dim=8: AUROC $= 0.8431 \pm 0.0007$ ($n=3$)
  \item \texttt{learned}, dim=16: AUROC $= 0.8428 \pm 0.0006$ ($n=3$)
  \item \texttt{learned}, dim=32: AUROC $= 0.8402 \pm 0.0004$ ($n=3$)
  \item \texttt{one\_hot}, num\_types: AUROC $= 0.8442 \pm 0.0006$ ($n=3$)
  \item \texttt{fixed\_random}, dim=8: AUROC $= 0.8435 \pm 0.0009$ ($n=3$)
  \item \texttt{fixed\_random}, dim=16: AUROC $= 0.8400 \pm 0.0020$ ($n=3$)
  \item \texttt{fixed\_random}, dim=32: AUROC $= 0.8397 \pm 0.0004$ ($n=3$)
\end{itemize}

The spread across all cells is $\sim 0.004$ AUROC, which is within two standard deviations of the seed spread for individual cells.
No mode-dimension combination is strongly superior to any other at $n=3$ seeds.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.80\textwidth]{figures/ch4/figure-auroc_heatmap_pid_mode_x_embed_dim.pdf}
\caption{Mean test AUROC as a function of PID embedding mode (T1-a) and embedding dimension (T1-b), for the \texttt{identity} tokenizer.
\texttt{one\_hot} is invariant to dimension by construction (dimension is set to the number of particle types).
All cells show similar performance; the total spread is $\sim 0.004$ AUROC.}
\label{fig:heatmap-pid-dim}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The result suggests that the model is not strongly sensitive to whether the PID label is encoded as a trainable embedding, a fixed random projection, or a discrete one-hot vector.
All three modes provide a representation that is sufficiently distinct per particle type for the transformer to exploit.
The \texttt{one\_hot} mode at dimension equal to the number of particle types ($\text{num\_types}$) is the most parameter-efficient choice, as it requires no additional trainable weights and is insensitive to the embedding dimension hyperparameter.
This mode is adopted as the baseline for subsequent chapters, paired with the default dimension setting.

\section{Data Treatment}
\label{sec:ch4-data}
% ============================================================

Having established the tokenizer family and PID encoding, this section examines three choices about the content and arrangement of the token sequence: whether energy is included in the feature vector (\wandbaxis{D01}), whether MET enters as an additional token (\wandbaxis{D02}), and whether tokens are presented in their original reconstruction order or shuffled (\wandbaxis{D03}).
All results in this section are from Experiment~4B (Table~\ref{tab:exp4b}), which uses the identity tokenizer with T1-a\,=\,\texttt{learned} and T1-b\,=\,8 throughout.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Energy Feature (D01)}
\label{sec:ch4-energy}
% -------------------------------------------------------

The feature-set axis (\wandbaxis{D01}) compares two configurations: the full four-vector $(E, p_T, \eta, \phi)$ with index set \texttt{[0,1,2,3]}, and the three-vector $(p_T, \eta, \phi)$ with index set \texttt{[1,2,3]}, which omits the energy component.

Figure~\ref{fig:auroc-bar-features} shows the result.
Removing energy yields AUROC $= 0.8424 \pm 0.0010$ versus $0.8431 \pm 0.0007$ with energy, a difference of $-0.0007$.
This difference is smaller than the within-condition standard deviation and cannot be distinguished from seed noise at $n=3$.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{figures/ch4/figure-auroc_bar_by_features.pdf}
\caption{Mean test AUROC by feature set (D01) for the identity tokenizer.
\texttt{[0,1,2,3]}: full four-vector $(E, p_T, \eta, \phi)$.
\texttt{[1,2,3]}: three-vector $(p_T, \eta, \phi)$ without energy.
The difference ($-0.0007$) is below the seed-spread level and constitutes a null result.}
\label{fig:auroc-bar-features}
\end{figure}

The null result is physically plausible: for massless or near-massless particles, energy is approximately $E \approx p_T \cosh \eta$, so $p_T$ and $\eta$ together recover much of the energy information.
The genuinely mass-sensitive component — relevant for reconstructing invariant masses of top decay sub-systems — may not be detectable at this model capacity and sample size.
The full four-vector (\texttt{[0,1,2,3]}) is retained in the chapter baseline because it carries no performance cost and preserves the option to use the nodewise mass module (P1) in subsequent chapters, which requires the energy feature.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{MET Treatment (D02)}
\label{sec:ch4-met}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Missing transverse energy (MET) represents the net transverse momentum carried by particles that are not detected, primarily neutrinos from $W$-boson decays.
In 4-tops events, which contain at least four top quarks with multiple semi-leptonic or fully hadronic decay chains, MET provides information about the neutrino system.
The MET treatment axis (\wandbaxis{D02}) tests whether including MET as an additional token improves classification.

Figure~\ref{fig:auroc-bar-met} shows the marginal comparison across D01 and D03.
With MET included (D02\,=\,\texttt{true}), the mean AUROC is $0.8423 \pm 0.0019$ ($n=12$); without MET, it is $0.8415 \pm 0.0017$ ($n=12$).
The difference is $+0.0008$ in favour of including MET, which is well within the pooled standard deviation and is not statistically distinguishable from zero at this sample size.
Restricting to the \texttt{input\_order} condition only (D03\,=\,\texttt{input\_order}, $n=6$ per MET condition), the delta is $+0.0007$, consistent with the full marginal result.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{figures/ch4/figure-auroc_bar_by_met.pdf}
\caption{Mean test AUROC by MET treatment (D02), marginalised over D01 and D03 conditions.
Including MET as a token yields a difference of $+0.0008$ AUROC, which is indistinguishable from noise at $n=12$ per condition.
This is a null result.}
\label{fig:auroc-bar-met}
\end{figure}

This is a null result: neither benefit nor harm from MET inclusion is measurable in Experiment~4B.
The result is inconsistent with an earlier, superseded figure of $-0.026$ that appeared in a prior version of the analysis; that figure has been traced to a data-loading bug in the re-inference pipeline, in which the validation dataset was not correctly augmented with the MET token for MET=True models.
The correct values are those in Table~\ref{tab:exp4b-d02d03}, drawn directly from \texttt{eval\_v2/test\_auroc} as logged at training time.

The PE-disruption hypothesis — that MET, appended at a fixed token position, disrupts the sinusoidal positional encoding (E1) by occupying a slot that the model otherwise associates with a specific particle type — is a plausible post-hoc explanation that has not been tested experimentally.
Such a test would require a dedicated experiment crossing D02 with E1\,=\,\texttt{none} (no positional encoding) to isolate any PE-mediated effect.
No such experiment has been conducted.
MET is excluded from the chapter baseline (D02\,=\,\texttt{false}) primarily because it provides no measured benefit; its re-introduction via physics-informed attention biases (B1-G1\,=\,\texttt{met\_direction}) is examined in a subsequent chapter.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Token Ordering (D03)}
\label{sec:ch4-order}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Self-attention is permutation-equivariant by construction: without positional encoding, the output is invariant to any permutation of the input tokens.
However, all runs in this chapter use sinusoidal positional encoding (E1\,=\,\texttt{sinusoidal}) in model space (E1-a\,=\,\texttt{model}).
The token ordering axis (\wandbaxis{D03}) tests whether the choice between preserving the original reconstruction order (\texttt{input\_order}) and randomly shuffling the sequence (\texttt{shuffled}) affects AUROC.

Figure~\ref{fig:auroc-bar-shuffle} shows the marginal comparison restricted to D02\,=\,\texttt{false} conditions, which removes any potential interaction with the MET token.
\texttt{input\_order} yields a mean AUROC of $0.8428 \pm 0.0008$ ($n=6$); \texttt{shuffled} yields $0.8402 \pm 0.0012$ ($n=6$).
The gap of $-0.0026$ (shuffled vs.\ input\_order) is approximately $2$–$3\times$ the standard deviation of the individual conditions and is consistent across both D01 values.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{figures/ch4/figure-auroc_bar_by_shuffle.pdf}
\caption{Mean test AUROC by token ordering (D03), for D02\,=\,\texttt{false} conditions ($n=6$ per bar).
Shuffled token sequences yield $-0.0026$ AUROC relative to the original reconstruction order.
The effect is consistent across feature-set conditions.}
\label{fig:auroc-bar-shuffle}
\end{figure}

Table~\ref{tab:exp4b-d02d03} shows the full $2\times2\times2$ factorial mean AUROC table for all D01, D02, D03 combinations.
The ordering effect is present under both MET conditions, suggesting it is not mediated by an interaction between D03 and D02.

\paragraph{Attribution.}
The ordering effect is consistent with the sinusoidal positional encoding providing useful information when tokens are presented in a consistent order, and that information being destroyed by random shuffling.
However, this attribution requires a control experiment with E1\,=\,\texttt{none} (no positional encoding) to confirm.
If the ordering effect disappears under E1\,=\,\texttt{none}, the effect is mediated by the positional encoding.
If it persists, the transformer's residual stream or the classification head is sensitive to token position through some other mechanism.
This control experiment has not been run.
The thesis states the ordering effect as a measured result and the PE attribution as a hypothesis.

The reconstruction software produces tokens in an order that is not $p_T$-sorted but reflects the detector reconstruction sequence, which may carry implicit correlations between position and particle type or kinematics.
If such correlations exist, the sinusoidal PE would be implicitly encoding a type-proxy, which is a legitimate, if implicit, inductive bias.
Characterising this ordering is a direction for future work.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:ch4-discussion}
% ============================================================

This section synthesises the results of Experiments~4A and~4B and establishes the baseline configuration for subsequent chapters.

\paragraph{Ranking of effects.}
The tokenizer family (\wandbaxis{T1}) dominates all other choices by a large margin.
The identity-versus-raw gap of $+0.038$ AUROC ($+0.117$ identity versus binned) is an order of magnitude larger than any data-treatment effect.
Within the identity family, PID mode (\wandbaxis{T1-a}) and dimension (\wandbaxis{T1-b}) effects are at most $\sim 0.004$ AUROC, comparable to seed-spread.
All three data-treatment axes produce effects below $0.003$ AUROC in this experiment; none is distinguishable from noise with certainty at $n=3$ seeds.

The narrative arc of this chapter is therefore: \emph{choose the identity tokenizer; thereafter, fine-grained choices do not matter at this model scale and seed count.}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Three limitations are worth stating explicitly.

First, only three seeds per condition are available.
The D03 ordering effect ($-0.0026$) is the largest of the data-treatment results and is suggestive of a real effect, but would require $n \geq 6$ per condition to approach 95\% confidence given typical seed-spread variability of $\sim 0.001$ AUROC.
The D01 and D02 results ($<0.001$) are almost certainly null results, but cannot be proven so with the current seed count.

Second, the attribution of the D03 ordering effect to the sinusoidal positional encoding has not been confirmed.
An E1\,=\,\texttt{none} control is the minimum experiment required.
Similarly, the PE-disruption hypothesis for D02 is untested.

Third, Experiment~4B uses T1-a\,=\,\texttt{learned} and T1-b\,=\,8 as the fixed tokenizer configuration, which is not the best-performing individual cell identified in Experiment~4A.
The D02 and D03 effects may differ under \texttt{one\_hot} tokenization; this cross-check has not been performed.

\paragraph{Chapter baseline.}
Table~\ref{tab:ch4-baseline} records the axis values chosen as the baseline for all subsequent chapters.
Choices follow the criterion of maximising expected performance while minimising hyperparameter sensitivity.
