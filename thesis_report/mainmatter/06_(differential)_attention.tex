\chapter{Attention Mechanisms}
\label{ch:attention}

The attention mechanism is the only component that simultaneously relates every pair
of particle tokens. Chapters~4 and~5 asked how to enrich the information arriving at
the attention layer; this chapter asks whether the attention computation itself should
be modified. Three axes are investigated: the \emph{type} of attention
(\wandbaxis{A3}), an optional \emph{attention-internal normalisation}
(\wandbaxis{A4}), and the \emph{mode in which a physics bias interacts with the two
branches of differential attention} (\wandbaxis{A3-a}). The Lorentz-scalar bias axis
\wandbaxis{B1} from Chapter~5 reappears here as a co-factor, because the interaction
between physics bias and attention type is the primary scientific question of
Section~\ref{sec:ch6_attn_bias}.

The physics motivation is direct. In a multi-top event, only a small subset of the
10--20 reconstructed objects are decay products of the four top quarks; standard
softmax attention distributes probability mass across all token pairs, including
unrelated soft objects. Differential attention~\cite{YE2025}
suppresses diffuse attention by computing the normalised difference of two softmax
maps, providing a structurally motivated inductive bias for signal concentration.
Whether this mechanism transfers from the natural-language setting to particle
classification is a testable hypothesis addressed in Section~\ref{sec:ch6_attn_type}.

Two experiments were run. Experiment~6A establishes the headline comparison across
attention type (\wandbaxis{A3}), internal normalisation (\wandbaxis{A4}), and the
presence of a Lorentz-scalar bias (\wandbaxis{B1}). Experiment~6B is a targeted
follow-up on how the Lorentz-scalar bias should interact with the two differential
attention branches (\wandbaxis{A3-a}), with attention type and normalisation fixed at
the values recommended by Exp.~6A. Section~\ref{sec:ch6_summary} synthesises the
findings and states the recommended configuration carried forward to subsequent
chapters.

\section{Differential Attention}
\label{sec:ch6_diff_attn}

Standard multi-head self-attention computes, for each head $h$, a single softmax map
over scaled dot products:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Attn}_h(\mathbf{Q},\mathbf{K},\mathbf{V})
    = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}_h \mathbf{K}_h^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right)\mathbf{V}_h.
\end{equation}
The output mixes content from all positions weighted by the softmax distribution, which
is strictly positive and therefore cannot cancel spurious correlations. Differential
attention~\cite{YE2025} replaces this with the difference of
two independent softmax maps computed on non-overlapping halves of the query and key
projections:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{DiffAttn}_h(\mathbf{Q},\mathbf{K},\mathbf{V})
    = \bigl[\mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{Q}_{h1}\mathbf{K}_{h1}^\top/\sqrt{d_h})
            - \lambda_h\,\mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{Q}_{h2}\mathbf{K}_{h2}^\top/\sqrt{d_h})\bigr]
      \mathbf{V}_h,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_h \in \mathbb{R}^{+}$ is a per-head scalar initialised to a small
positive value and learned during training. The subtraction cancels attention patterns
common to both maps, amplifying the response to pairs that are relevant to one map but
not the other. The mechanism doubles the query and key projection cost per head but
keeps value dimension and model depth unchanged; the additional parameter burden is
minimal (one scalar $\lambda_h$ per head per layer).

In the particle-physics context, the differential mechanism is expected to assign near-zero
net attention weight to object pairs that are kinematically inert with respect to
the classification decision, while concentrating weight on pairs with discriminating
invariant-mass or angular structure. The Lorentz-scalar bias (\wandbaxis{B1}) provides
complementary guidance by adding a physics-motivated additive term to the attention
logits before the softmax; the interaction between the two is a central question of
this chapter.

The 36 runs span all $2 \times 3 \times 2$ cells with 3 seeds each. Performance was

also recorded to quantify the overhead of the differential attention pass.

\subsection{Results: Attention Type}
\label{subsec:ch6_attn_type_results}

Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_A3_seedspread} shows the seed-averaged AUROC for all 36 Exp.~6A
configurations, grouped by attention type. Differential attention consistently
outperforms standard attention across all normalisation and bias conditions.
Marginalised over all 45 chapter-6 runs, the mean AUROC of
\wandbaxis{A3}${}=\texttt{differential}$ is $0.831 \pm 0.003$ ($n=27$) versus
$0.827 \pm 0.003$ ($n=18$) for \texttt{standard}, a difference of $+0.0036$ absolute
($+0.36$\,pp). The gap is small but present with consistent sign across seed replicas.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{figures/ch6/ch6_A3_auroc_seedspread_attn_type.pdf}

    normalisation and bias combination.}
  \label{fig:ch6_A3_seedspread}
\end{figure}

The per-class decomposition in Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_A3_per_class} confirms the gain is
not concentrated in a single class. Differential attention improves AUROC for all five
signal categories, with the largest absolute gain in the $t\bar{t}H$ channel
($+0.0045$) and the smallest in $t\bar{t}WW$ ($+0.0034$). The $t\bar{t}WW$ channel is
consistently the hardest class (AUROC $\approx 0.737$ for standard and $0.738$ for
differential), reflecting the kinematic similarity between four-top and
double-$W$-associated production at high jet multiplicity.

\begin{figure}[h]

Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_A3_val_curves} shows validation AUROC curves during training.

cancellation strength is learned smoothly over training, converging to stable non-zero
values. This confirms that the differential mechanism is active and not degenerate
(a degenerate case, $\lambda_h \to 0$, would recover standard attention).

\subsection{Results: Attention-Internal Normalisation}
\label{subsec:ch6_attn_norm_results}

Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_A4_seedspread} shows AUROC grouped by \wandbaxis{A4}. Applying
LayerNorm or RMSNorm inside the attention module consistently reduces mean AUROC
compared to no normalisation. The means are $0.832 \pm 0.002$ for
\texttt{none} ($n=21$), $0.827 \pm 0.002$ for \texttt{layernorm} ($n=12$), and
$0.826 \pm 0.003$ for \texttt{rmsnorm} ($n=12$). The gap of approximately $0.005$
absolute ($0.5$\,pp) is present for both attention types and both bias conditions,
as shown in the interaction heatmap of Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_A3xA4_heatmap}.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{figures/ch6/ch6_A4_auroc_seedspread_attn_norm.pdf}
  \caption{Seed-spread AUROC for the three A4 attention-internal normalisation
    options (\texttt{none}, \texttt{layernorm}, \texttt{rmsnorm}),
    marginalised over A3 and B1.
    No normalisation is consistently best; LayerNorm and RMSNorm are nearly
    equivalent and both below the \texttt{none} baseline.}
  \label{fig:ch6_A4_seedspread}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{figures/ch6/ch6_A3xA4_auroc_heatmap.pdf}
  \caption{Mean AUROC heatmap for the A3 $\times$ A4 interaction.
    The \texttt{none} normalisation row is best for both attention types.
    Differential attention (left column) consistently outperforms standard (right

  \label{fig:ch6_A3xA4_heatmap}

A plausible explanation is that attention-internal normalisation re-scales the
raw dot-product values against which the Lorentz-scalar bias is calibrated,
reducing the effective contribution of the physics term. An alternative explanation
is that the normalisation layer introduces additional smoothing that is unhelpful in
a relatively shallow architecture (six encoder layers). The evidence is insufficient
to distinguish these explanations, but the recommendation is unambiguous:
\wandbaxis{A4}${}=\texttt{none}$ throughout.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_A4_val_curves} confirms that the ordering is stable over training
and is not a transient artefact of convergence differences.

\begin{figure}[h]

    the gap to the normalised variants is established within the first 20 epochs

\section{Physics Bias and Differential Attention}
\label{sec:ch6_attn_bias}

\subsection{Experimental Design}
\label{subsec:ch6_bias_design}

The Lorentz-scalar bias (\wandbaxis{B1}) was already present as a co-factor in
Exp.~6A; this section analyses its interaction with attention type. Section~\ref{subsec:ch6_diffbias_design}
then introduces Exp.~6B, which studies how the bias interacts with the two
softmax branches of differential attention via axis \wandbaxis{A3-a}.

\subsection{Results: B1 by Attention Type}
\label{subsec:ch6_bias_results}

Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_B1_seedspread} summarises the marginalised B1 effect. The
Lorentz-scalar bias provides a mean AUROC gain of $+0.0025$ ($+0.25$\,pp) over the
45 chapter-6 runs ($0.830 \pm 0.004$ with bias, $0.828 \pm 0.003$ without).

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{figures/ch6/ch6_B1_auroc_seedspread_bias.pdf}
  \caption{Seed-spread AUROC for B1 bias options (\texttt{none} vs.\
    \texttt{lorentz\_scalar}), marginalised over A3 and A4.
    The Lorentz-scalar bias provides a modest positive shift; the effect
    is larger for differential than for standard attention
    (see Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_A3xB1_heatmap}).}
  \label{fig:ch6_B1_seedspread}
\end{figure}

The critical finding is the A3 $\times$ B1 interaction shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_A3xB1_heatmap}. For standard attention the Lorentz-scalar bias
provides essentially no benefit: the mean AUROC with and without the bias is
$0.827$ in both cases (absolute difference $+0.0002$, well within the seed spread).
For differential attention the same bias yields a mean gain of $+0.0033$. This
interaction suggests that the learnable $\lambda_h$ parameter of differential
attention can modulate how strongly the physics bias enters the final attention score:
when the raw dot-product difference already suppresses uninformative pairs, the additive
physics term can concentrate further on the most discriminating pairwise features.
Standard attention lacks this modulation and appears unable to exploit the bias
structure consistently.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{figures/ch6/ch6_A3xB1_auroc_heatmap.pdf}
  \caption{Mean AUROC heatmap for the A3 $\times$ B1 interaction,
    marginalised over A4 (Exp.~6A runs only).
    The Lorentz-scalar bias provides a clear gain for differential attention
    ($+0.0033$) but is ineffective for standard attention ($+0.0002$).}
  \label{fig:ch6_A3xB1_heatmap}
\end{figure}

Within the cleanest A4${}=\texttt{none}$ subgroup (Table~\ref{tab:ch6_A3xB1_clean}),
the pattern holds: \texttt{standard+lorentz\_scalar} ($0.832$) reaches approximately
the same level as \texttt{differential+none} ($0.831$), while
\texttt{differential+lorentz\_scalar} ($0.833$) is highest. The bias and the
differential mechanism therefore contribute independently and in an approximately
additive manner.

\section{Differential Bias Mode}
\label{sec:ch6_diff_bias_mode}

\subsection{Experimental Design}
\label{subsec:ch6_diffbias_design}

Differential attention has two softmax branches; a physics-informed additive bias can
be applied to both branches identically (\texttt{shared}), distributed to each branch
via independent network heads with separate parameters (\texttt{split}), or omitted
entirely (\texttt{none}). Axis \wandbaxis{A3-a} controls this choice.

Experiment~6B swept \wandbaxis{A3-a} with A3${}=\texttt{differential}$,
A4${}=\texttt{none}$, and B1${}=\texttt{lorentz\_scalar}$ fixed at the values
recommended by Exp.~6A.

\subsection{Results}
\label{subsec:ch6_diffbias_results}

Figure~\ref{fig:ch6_A3a_seedspread} and Table~\ref{tab:ch6_A3a_results} summarise
the Exp.~6B outcomes. All three A3-a configurations perform closely: \texttt{split}
achieves a mean AUROC of $0.8330 \pm 0.0016$ ($n=3$), \texttt{none} of
$0.8326 \pm 0.0022$ ($n=3$), and \texttt{shared} of $0.8300 \pm 0.0027$ ($n=21$,
including Exp.~6A runs with varying A4 and B1 conditions). The differences among the
three modes do not exceed one standard deviation of the seed spread and should be
considered negligible.

The \texttt{split} mode provides two independent MLP heads to produce the two
attention-logit additive terms — one per branch — whereas \texttt{shared} adds the
same bias to both branches. The near-identical performance of \texttt{split} and
\texttt{none} (relative to \texttt{shared}) suggests that the second branch does not
strongly benefit from an independent bias signal. A practical consequence is that the
choice of A3-a can be made on the basis of model complexity preference: \texttt{split}
doubles the parameters of the Lorentz-scalar MLP but provides no measurable gain over
\texttt{shared} at fixed seed count. The \texttt{split} mode is adopted as the default
for subsequent chapters, acknowledging that the difference is within noise.

A methodological note on the A3-a confounder: Exp.~6B was designed with
B1${}=\texttt{lorentz\_scalar}$ fixed, so the effect of A3-a when B1${}=\texttt{none}$
is not measured. The A3-a effect is therefore not fully separable from B1 within this
dataset.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:ch6_summary}

The three main findings are as follows.

\textbf{Differential attention improves classification performance.}
Axis \wandbaxis{A3}${}=\texttt{differential}$ yields a mean AUROC gain of
$+0.0036$ over \texttt{standard} attention, with consistent positive sign
across all five signal classes, all normalisation settings, and three random
seeds. The $\lambda_h$ parameters converge to stable non-zero values, confirming
that the cancellation mechanism is active.

\textbf{Attention-internal normalisation is detrimental.}
Axis \wandbaxis{A4}${}=\texttt{none}$ is best in all (A3, B1) sub-groups.
Both LayerNorm and RMSNorm reduce mean AUROC by approximately $0.005$ absolute.
The effect is consistent in direction across all conditions.

\textbf{The Lorentz-scalar bias is effective only with differential attention.}
Axis \wandbaxis{B1}${}=\texttt{lorentz\_scalar}$ provides a gain of $+0.0033$
for differential attention but essentially no gain ($+0.0002$) for standard attention.
The two mechanisms are approximately additive: the best configuration in this chapter
(\wandbaxis{A3}${}=\texttt{differential}$, \wandbaxis{A4}${}=\texttt{none}$,
\wandbaxis{B1}${}=\texttt{lorentz\_scalar}$, \wandbaxis{A3-a}${}=\texttt{split}$)
achieves a mean AUROC of $0.8330 \pm 0.0016$, compared to the baseline
(\texttt{standard}, A4${}=\texttt{none}$, B1${}=\texttt{none}$) at
$0.8296 \pm 0.0027$ — an absolute gain of $+0.0034$ ($+0.34$\,pp).
